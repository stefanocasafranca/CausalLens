\title{CausalLens: An Open-Source Toolkit for Causal Autonomy Auditing of Recommender Systems}

\author{Anonymous Author(s)}

\begin{abstract}
Observational metrics such as intra-list diversity and recommendation volatility are widely used to assess whether recommender systems respect user autonomy. We show these metrics carry essentially no information about whether users can actually change their recommendations. Using perturbation-based estimates of interventional quantities from the causal auditing framework of Sharma et al.\ (2024), we find that observational autonomy classifications are statistically independent of causal reachability for traditional collaborative filtering models ($\chi^2$ tests, $p > 0.4$ for MF and NeuMF conditions). We introduce \textbf{CausalLens}, an open-source Python toolkit that operationalizes this framework into four computable metrics: Reachability Cost, Manipulation Resistance, Autonomy Asymmetry Index (AAI), and Observational Deception Rate (ODR). CausalLens provides both white-box gradient-based and black-box query-based computation paths, supports any recommender exposing a predict/update interface, and reproduces our full experimental pipeline on a single laptop. Our ablation across four architectures---MF, NeuMF, LightGCN, and SASRec---on two datasets ($N = 1{,}600$ user evaluations) reveals qualitatively distinct autonomy profiles: neural models (NeuMF) amplify manipulation vulnerability (rank-biserial $r = 0.50$, $p < 10^{-34}$) while graph-based models (LightGCN) exhibit extreme structural rigidity, and sequential models (SASRec) produce the highest reachability rates. Code and data are publicly available.\footnote{Anonymous repository: \url{https://anonymous.4open.science/r/CausalLens} (for review only).}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ═════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Recommender systems can propagate influence across users in unexpected ways. Calandrino et al.~\cite{calandrino2011you} demonstrated that an observer can infer a target user's private transactions from temporal changes in a recommender's public outputs---a consequence of collaborative filtering's cross-user parameter sharing. More broadly, one user's behavior can alter what another user sees, yet no tool existed to measure this cross-user influence, quantify its magnitude, or determine whether the affected user could escape it through their own actions.

This cross-user influence channel illustrates a fundamental limitation of current recommender system auditing. Regulators and researchers rely on \emph{observational} metrics---diversity of recommended items, catalog coverage, recommendation volatility---to assess whether systems respect user autonomy~\cite{eu_dsa2022, ge2010beyond, zhang2008avoiding}. These metrics capture surface-level properties of recommendation lists but cannot distinguish between a user who genuinely controls their feed and one whose apparent autonomy is an artifact of the system's internal dynamics.

Sharma et al.~\cite{sharma2024unified} formalized this distinction through a causal framework for auditing recommender systems, defining metrics grounded in interventional reasoning: what happens when a user \emph{tries} to change their recommendations, and what happens when an adversary \emph{tries} to change them for someone else. Their framework provides the theoretical foundation, but no reproducible implementation exists. We operationalize their definitions through perturbation-and-retrain experiments that provide lower bounds on manipulation displacement and upper bounds on reachability cost---tractable approximations to the underlying interventional quantities.

\textbf{CausalLens} fills this gap. We contribute:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item An \textbf{open-source toolkit} operationalizing four causal autonomy metrics with both white-box and black-box computation paths;
    \item \textbf{Reachability Cost} and \textbf{Manipulation Resistance} formulated as constrained optimization problems with gradient-based and query-based solvers;
    \item Two composite metrics---\textbf{Autonomy Asymmetry Index} (AAI) and \textbf{Observational Deception Rate} (ODR)---that operationalize the gap between observational and causal assessments into single scalar indicators;
    \item The \textbf{first empirical ablation} of perturbation-based vs.\ observational auditing across four architecture families---linear~(MF), neural~(NeuMF), graph-based~(LightGCN), and sequential~(SASRec)---on two datasets, revealing qualitatively distinct autonomy profiles across model classes.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%% ═════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\paragraph{Causal auditing theory.}
Sharma et al.~\cite{sharma2024unified} proposed a unified causal framework for auditing recommender systems, formalizing notions of reachability, manipulation, and autonomy using Pearl's causal inference machinery~\cite{pearl2009causality}. Their work establishes the theoretical foundations we operationalize. CausalLens translates their definitions into computable algorithms with both gradient-based (white-box) and query-based (black-box) solvers, and conducts the first large-scale empirical evaluation of causal vs.\ observational auditing.

\paragraph{Reachability in recommender systems.}
Dean et al.~\cite{dean2020recommendations} introduced the concept of reachability in collaborative filtering, asking whether users can reach arbitrary items through rating changes. Curmei et al.~\cite{curmei2021towards} extended this with psychologically-grounded preference dynamics. CausalLens builds on these foundations by (i) coupling reachability with adversarial manipulation resistance into a unified audit, and (ii) introducing the ODR metric that exposes the gap between observational and causal assessments.

\paragraph{Steerability and user agency.}
Recent work has examined steerability from complementary angles. Tsai and Brusilovsky~\cite{tsai2021controllability} studied the effects of user controllability in social recommender systems, finding that interactive controls improve perceived quality. Jannach et al.~\cite{jannach2017user} surveyed user control mechanisms in recommender interfaces. These approaches focus on interface-level interventions rather than quantitative measurement of whether a user's actions \emph{actually change} the underlying recommendations---the question CausalLens addresses.

\paragraph{Regulatory auditing.}
The EU Digital Services Act~\cite{eu_dsa2022} requires platforms to assess systemic risks including effects on fundamental rights, creating demand for rigorous auditing methodology. Early analyses of platform audit reports have identified significant methodological gaps in how algorithmic influence is assessed---gaps that CausalLens addresses with concrete causal tooling.

\paragraph{Observational metrics.}
Intra-list diversity~\cite{zhang2008avoiding}, catalog coverage~\cite{ge2010beyond}, and recommendation volatility are standard beyond-accuracy metrics. Our ODR metric demonstrates that these observational indicators can be systematically misleading as proxies for user autonomy.

\paragraph{Cross-user dynamics.}
Chaney et al.~\cite{chaney2018algorithmic} demonstrated that collaborative filtering creates feedback loops amplifying homogeneity across users---an effect our manipulation resistance metric quantifies at the individual level. Mansoury et al.~\cite{mansoury2020feedback} showed that such loops systematically bias recommendation outputs over time. CausalLens complements these population-level observations with per-user causal measurements of cross-user influence.

\section{Formal Definitions}
\label{sec:definitions}
%% ═════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Our operationalization approximates causal interventions through controlled perturbation and retraining. Rather than constructing an explicit structural causal model with identified confounders, we simulate $do$-interventions by modifying a user's ratings (or an adversary's ratings) and measuring the downstream effect on recommendations after model update. This perturbation-based approach provides a tractable approximation to the interventional quantities defined by Sharma et al.~\cite{sharma2024unified}, trading full causal identification for computational feasibility and model-agnostic applicability. Our metrics measure \emph{perturbation sensitivity under controlled retraining}, which lower-bounds the true causal effect.

Let $\mathcal{U} = \{1, \ldots, n\}$ be the set of users, $\mathcal{V} = \{1, \ldots, m\}$ the set of items, $\mathbf{r}_i \in \mathbb{R}^m$ user $i$'s rating vector, $R \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times m}$ the full rating matrix, $f$ the recommender function, $\text{top-}k(f, i)$ the set of $k$ recommended items for user $i$, and $d(\cdot, \cdot)$ the Jaccard distance between two top-$k$ sets.

\begin{definition}[Reachability Cost]
\label{def:reachability}
The reachability cost for user $i$ to reach item $j$ in the top-$k$ is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{R}(i, j, k) = \min_{\delta} \|\delta\|_1 \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \text{rank}(f, j, i \mid \mathbf{r}_i + \delta) \leq k, \; \delta \in \mathcal{C}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{C}$ constrains perturbations to valid rating values applied to user $i$'s \emph{own} ratings.
\end{definition}

\noindent \textbf{Intuition.} Reachability cost measures how many of their own ratings a user must change to push a target item into their top-$k$. High cost means the user is ``stuck''---unable to meaningfully steer their recommendations.

\begin{definition}[Manipulation Resistance]
\label{def:manipulation}
The manipulation resistance of user $i$ against adversary $a$ with budget $\varepsilon$ is:
\begin{multline}
    \mathcal{M}(i, a, \varepsilon) = \max_{\delta_a} d\!\bigl(\text{top-}k(f, i \mid R), \; \text{top-}k(f, i \mid R + \Delta_a)\bigr) \\
    \text{s.t.} \quad \|\delta_a\|_1 \leq \varepsilon
\end{multline}
where $\Delta_a$ modifies \emph{only} adversary $a$'s ratings.
\end{definition}

\noindent \textbf{Intuition.} Manipulation resistance quantifies how much a stranger can shift a victim's recommendations by changing only their own ratings. Low resistance (high displacement) means the user's feed is vulnerable to cross-user influence.

\begin{definition}[Autonomy Asymmetry Index]
\label{def:aai}
Let $S(i, \varepsilon) = d(\text{top-}k(f, i \mid R), \text{top-}k(f, i \mid R + \Delta_i^*))$ be the self-influence displacement (maximum shift from the user's own budget-$\varepsilon$ perturbation), and $E(i, \varepsilon) = \max_a \mathcal{M}(i, a, \varepsilon)$ the maximum adversary displacement. The Autonomy Asymmetry Index is:
\begin{equation}
    \text{AAI}(i, \varepsilon) = \frac{E(i, \varepsilon)}{S(i, \varepsilon)}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\noindent \textbf{Intuition.} AAI $< 1$ means the user has more influence over their own feed than any adversary (healthy). AAI $> 1$ means an external actor can move the user's recommendations more than the user themselves can (problematic).

\begin{definition}[Observational Deception Rate]
\label{def:odr}
Let $\mathcal{A} \subset \mathcal{U}$ be the set of users flagged as autonomous by observational criteria (diversity above median \emph{and} volatility above median, computed within each model--dataset combination). Let $\mathcal{T} \subset \mathcal{U}$ be users who are causally trapped (reachability success rate $= 0$). The Observational Deception Rate is:
\begin{equation}
    \text{ODR} = \frac{|\mathcal{A} \cap \mathcal{T}|}{|\mathcal{A}|}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\noindent \textbf{Intuition.} ODR measures the fraction of users who \emph{look} autonomous by standard metrics but cannot change their recommendations through their own actions. High ODR means observational auditing systematically fails to detect causal entrapment.

We say a user is \textit{causally trapped} if their reachability success rate is zero under budget~$B$---no target item can be placed into their top-$k$ through any combination of up to~$B$ rating changes. This is a budget-relative property: a user trapped under $B = 20$ may become reachable under a larger budget.

\section{System Design}
\label{sec:system}
%% ═════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

CausalLens is a Python library built around an abstract \texttt{Recom\-mend\-er} interface with three methods: \texttt{get\_scores} (raw scores), \texttt{get\_recom\-men\-da\-tions} (top-$k$ retrieval), and \texttt{fit} (training). This abstraction lets CausalLens audit arbitrary recommender architectures without modifying the underlying model code.

\paragraph{Computation paths.}
CausalLens provides two computation paths for each metric:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{White-box (gradient-based):} For differentiable models (MF, NeuMF), reachability uses two-phase projected gradient descent---first maximizing the target item's score, then binary-searching the minimum budget. Manipulation resistance uses gradient ascent on the adversary's ratings to maximize Jaccard displacement. The MF scoring path uses a differentiable soft-sigmoid weighted ridge regression: $w_j = \sigma((r_j - 0.5) \cdot 20)$, ensuring unrated items receive near-zero weight while perturbations smoothly introduce new ratings.
    \item \textbf{Black-box (query-based):} For non-differentiable or API-only models, reachability uses greedy coordinate search (in-place modify/restore, avoiding matrix copies), and manipulation resistance uses hill-climbing with random restarts.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Cross-user coupling.}
For models with shared parameters, adversary perturbations trigger a warm-start retrain of shared embeddings, propagating effects to all users' scores. NeuMF uses 25 SGD steps on shared parameters plus 15 fine-tuning steps; MF uses 25-step warm-start retraining of shared item factors. LightGCN and SASRec use 10-step warm-start retrains; LightGCN additionally rebuilds its normalized adjacency matrix to reflect structural changes (edge additions/removals).

\paragraph{Pipeline.}
The end-to-end audit pipeline: load data $\rightarrow$ train model $\rightarrow$ select users $\rightarrow$ compute per-user metrics (reachability, manipulation, AAI, observational baselines) $\rightarrow$ compute ODR $\rightarrow$ aggregate and report. The full experiment reproduces on a single laptop via \texttt{python run\_all.py}.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}
%% ═════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\paragraph{Models.}
We evaluate four architectures spanning distinct structural classes, all implemented within CausalLens:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Matrix Factorization (MF)}~\cite{koren2009matrix}: 64-dimensional user/item factors trained with Adam for 20 epochs (batch size 1024). MF's linear structure provides a baseline: adversary perturbations trigger 25-step warm-start retrains of shared factors, but the linear model limits how far cross-user effects propagate.
    \item \textbf{Neural Collaborative Filtering (NeuMF)}~\cite{he2017neural}: GMF (32-dim) + MLP (64$\to$32$\to$16) dual-path architecture, 15 epochs, batch size 1024. NeuMF shares item embeddings across users; adversary perturbations trigger 25-step warm-start retrains of shared parameters, creating measurable cross-user coupling.
    \item \textbf{LightGCN}~\cite{he2020lightgcn}: 3-layer graph convolution over the user--item bipartite graph with 64-dimensional embeddings, 20 epochs (batch size 2048). LightGCN propagates information through the normalized adjacency matrix $\tilde{A} = D^{-1/2}AD^{-1/2}$; its scoring depends on graph \emph{structure} (edge presence) rather than rating values, producing high structural rigidity.
    \item \textbf{SASRec}~\cite{kang2018self}: 2-layer self-attention with 2 heads over length-50 interaction sequences, 64-dimensional embeddings, 30 epochs (batch size 256). SASRec conditions on the \emph{order} of user interactions via causal masking, making recommendations sensitive to which items a user has rated but not to rating values.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Datasets.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{MovieLens-1M}~\cite{harper2015movielens}: 6,040 users, 3,706 items, 1M explicit ratings (1--5).
    \item \textbf{Amazon Musical Instruments}~\cite{mcauley2015image}: 5-core subset with explicit ratings from the Amazon Reviews 2023 corpus.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Protocol.}
For each of the eight model--dataset combinations, we sample 200 users with $\geq 20$ ratings ($N = 1{,}600$ total evaluations). Per user: 5 reachability targets (random unrated items outside current top-$k$) with budget $B = 20$ rating changes; 3 random adversaries with budget $\varepsilon = 5$; self-influence with budget $\varepsilon = 5$. All models use the same black-box random-search solver to ensure cross-model comparability. For graph-based (LightGCN) and sequential (SASRec) models, whose scoring depends on edge presence rather than rating values, the self-influence solver mixes three action types: modifying existing ratings, adding new ratings (creating edges), and removing ratings (deleting edges). Observational autonomy thresholds are set at within-combo medians of diversity and volatility. All experiments use $k = 10$ and Jaccard distance.

\paragraph{Budget justification.}
The reachability budget $B = 20$ represents a substantial behavioral change; Section~\ref{sec:results} shows most targets remain unreachable, which is itself informative about structural rigidity.

\paragraph{Warm-start sensitivity.}
The warm-start retrain budget (25 steps for MF/NeuMF, 10 steps for LightGCN/SASRec) balances computational cost against coupling fidelity. Fewer steps underestimate cross-user propagation; full retraining to convergence yields an upper bound but is prohibitively expensive at scale ($N \times$ adversary evaluations per user). LightGCN and SASRec use fewer retrain steps because their inference cost per step is higher (graph propagation and attention, respectively).

\subsection{Observational Autonomy Is Orthogonal to Causal Reachability}

The relationship between observational autonomy labels and causal reachability varies qualitatively across architectures (Table~\ref{tab:main-results}). For MF and NeuMF, ODR closely tracks the base unreachability rate (ratios 0.97--1.00$\times$; $\chi^2$ tests non-significant, $p > 0.43$), confirming that diversity-and-volatility labels carry \emph{no discriminative signal} for reachability. On MF/ML-1M, 39 of 41 observationally autonomous users are trapped---but so are 190 of 200 users overall (95.0\%).

LightGCN produces zero volatility on both datasets, collapsing the observational autonomy classifier entirely: no users are flagged as autonomous, making ODR undefined (0/0). SASRec/ML-1M reveals a third pattern: observational autonomy is \emph{anti-correlated} with trapping ($\chi^2 = 99.7$, $p < 10^{-4}$). Only 14.3\% of observationally autonomous SASRec users are trapped, versus 68.0\% base unreachability---the opposite of what observational metrics are presumed to indicate.

These three regimes---orthogonal (MF/NeuMF), degenerate (LightGCN), and anti-correlated (SASRec)---demonstrate that the failure of observational autonomy metrics is not limited to a single mode of breakdown. MF and NeuMF ODR remains above 89\% at both the 25th and 75th percentile thresholds (Table~\ref{tab:main-results}).

\subsection{Architecture Determines Vulnerability}

Manipulation displacement spans two orders of magnitude across architectures (Figure~\ref{fig:manipulation}): NeuMF (mean 0.644 across datasets) $>$ MF (0.482) $\gg$ SASRec (0.048) $>$ LightGCN (0.020). The NeuMF--MF gap is significant (Mann-Whitney $U = 40{,}058$, $p < 10^{-34}$, rank-biserial $r = 0.50$, a large effect), most pronounced on Amazon-MI ($r = 0.72$) where the sparser rating matrix amplifies adversary perturbation propagation through shared embeddings.

LightGCN and SASRec produce near-zero manipulation despite strikingly different reachability profiles. LightGCN's message-passing aggregation distributes each user's signal across many neighbours, diluting single-user perturbations. SASRec's sequential attention is anchored to the user's own history, insulating other users' recommendation lists from cross-user updates. Both models use warm-start retraining with 10 gradient steps after perturbation, but the architectural constraints inherently bound how far a single adversary's influence propagates.

\subsection{Reachability Under Conservative Budgets}

Overall, 30.6\% of users (490/1{,}600) achieve reachability success within budget-20 (Figure~\ref{fig:reachability}), but the distribution varies sharply across architectures and datasets. MF and NeuMF show consistently low reachability (4--7.5\%) on both datasets, while graph-based and sequential models exhibit a dramatic dataset interaction: LightGCN is rigid on ML-1M (0\% reachable) but fragile on Amazon-MI (100\%); SASRec shifts from 32\% to 91.5\%. The sparse Amazon-MI graph ($\rho{=}0.0015$) provides insufficient structural support for message-passing and attention, making recommendations trivially steerable.

Among reachable users on MF and NeuMF, mean reachability costs are comparable (MF: 8.86, NeuMF: 9.27 across datasets). LightGCN/Amazon-MI and SASRec/Amazon-MI achieve reachability at near-zero cost (mean 0.00), confirming that these models offer no resistance to preference steering in the sparse regime.

\subsection{Autonomy Asymmetry}

Users with AAI ${>}\,1$ (adversary influence exceeds self-influence) are concentrated in MF/ML-1M (9.1\%; Figure~\ref{fig:aai}), with SASRec/ML-1M second (4.0\%). NeuMF shows fewer AAI~$>1$ users (1.5\% on ML-1M, 3.5\% on Amazon-MI) despite higher absolute manipulation, because its cross-user coupling amplifies self-influence proportionally: shared-parameter updates triggered by a user's own ratings propagate back to that user's scores. LightGCN and SASRec on Amazon-MI exhibit near-zero AAI (means 0.005 and 0.053 respectively), consistent with their negligible manipulation displacement---the adversary influence numerator is essentially zero despite high reachability.

The scatter geometry (Figure~\ref{fig:aai}) reveals four distinct clusters: MF users spread along the self-influence axis with occasional adversary-dominated outliers; NeuMF pushes users into higher-external territory but with proportionally higher self-influence; LightGCN/Amazon-MI collapses to the origin; and SASRec occupies an intermediate band with moderate self-influence but low external influence.

\subsection{ODR Across Architectures and Datasets}

Figure~\ref{fig:odr} shows ODR across all eight combinations. MF and NeuMF exhibit uniformly high ODR (89.3--95.1\%), with MF slightly exceeding NeuMF on both datasets (Fisher's exact: $p = 0.46$ on ML-1M, $p = 1.00$ on Amazon-MI---not significant). The observational baselines (Table~\ref{tab:odr-breakdown}) confirm that NeuMF produces higher diversity (ILD: 0.837--0.999 vs.\ 0.733--0.983) and volatility (0.576--0.721 vs.\ 0.125--0.357), which \emph{should} signal greater autonomy---yet perturbation-based metrics reveal the opposite.

LightGCN produces zero volatility on both datasets, so no users are classified as observationally autonomous and ODR is undefined (0/0). SASRec/ML-1M yields the only intermediate ODR (14.3\%, 8/56), reflecting that most of its observationally autonomous users \emph{are} reachable---a case where the observational label accidentally correlates with genuine agency. On Amazon-MI, SASRec's near-zero volatility again collapses the classifier (0/0). The pattern across architectures reveals that ODR failure is not a single phenomenon: it can manifest as false confidence (MF/NeuMF), classifier collapse (LightGCN), or accidental correctness (SASRec/ML-1M).

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%% ═════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\paragraph{Implications for auditing practice.}
Our results challenge the common assumption that diversity and volatility metrics suffice for autonomy auditing. Under the EU Digital Services Act~\cite{eu_dsa2022}, platforms must assess ``systemic risks'' including effects on fundamental rights. Our finding that observational autonomy labels are statistically independent of causal reachability means these metrics provide no assurance about user agency---auditors using them cannot distinguish a system that respects autonomy from one that does not. CausalLens provides the complementary perturbation-based layer that regulation implicitly requires.

\paragraph{The architecture matters.}
The four architectures produce qualitatively distinct autonomy profiles: NeuMF amplifies cross-user vulnerability through nonlinear embedding coupling; MF creates a lower but non-trivial structural floor; LightGCN exhibits extreme density-dependent rigidity/fragility; and SASRec produces high reachability with minimal cross-user effects. These differences are invisible to observational metrics, which either collapse (LightGCN's zero volatility) or provide uniformly misleading signals (MF/NeuMF's high ODR). As production systems combine these architectural primitives, causal auditing becomes essential.

\paragraph{Architecture scope.}
Our evaluation spans four architectures representing distinct structural classes: MF (linear), NeuMF (nonlinear, shared embedding coupling), LightGCN (graph convolution~\cite{he2020lightgcn}), and SASRec (sequential attention~\cite{kang2018self}). This breadth reveals that architecture determines not just the \emph{magnitude} of autonomy metrics but their qualitative character: graph-based models exhibit structural rigidity (near-zero volatility and manipulation), while sequential models produce the highest reachability rates. Production systems may combine these architectural primitives, making multi-class auditing essential.

\paragraph{Threats to validity.}
\emph{Internal}: Our black-box solvers (random search, hill-climbing) provide lower bounds on true manipulation displacement and upper bounds on reachability cost---stronger solvers would shift the numbers but not the direction of our findings. The warm-start retrain (25 steps) may underestimate full-retrain coupling effects.
\emph{External}: While our four models span linear, neural, graph-based, and sequential classes, production systems may combine these primitives in deeper architectures. Our two datasets span different domains and sparsity levels but both use explicit ratings; implicit-feedback systems may behave differently.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Our reachability budget ($B = 20$) is a fixed absolute value that represents different intervention magnitudes depending on profile size: 100\% for a 20-rating user but only 2\% for a 1000-rating user. Per-user fractional budgets would be more defensible; we leave this calibration to future work. ODR thresholds at population medians are a natural choice but not the only valid one; we verified that ODR remains above 68\% across the 25th--75th percentile range (see Section~\ref{sec:results}). CausalLens currently requires model-level access (white-box or retrain-capable); pure API-only auditing with no retrain capability remains an open challenge. Our experiments use a single random seed; multi-seed variance analysis is planned for the camera-ready version.

\paragraph{Ethical considerations.}
CausalLens simulates adversarial manipulation on offline datasets with no impact on real users. However, the toolkit could in principle be used to identify and exploit manipulation vulnerabilities in deployed systems. We release CausalLens as an auditing tool and encourage its use for defensive purposes---identifying vulnerable users and hardening recommendation pipelines---rather than for attack development. This dual-use concern is inherent to all security auditing tools and does not, in our view, outweigh the transparency benefits.


%% ═════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ═════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

We presented CausalLens, an open-source toolkit that operationalizes perturbation-based autonomy auditing for recommender systems. Across eight model--dataset conditions spanning four architecture families (200 users each, $N = 1{,}600$), we demonstrated that observational autonomy labels based on diversity and volatility are statistically independent of causal reachability---a finding that renders standard observational auditing unreliable as a proxy for user agency. Our four-model comparison reveals qualitatively distinct autonomy profiles: neural architectures (NeuMF) amplify manipulation vulnerability while producing higher observational diversity, graph-based architectures (LightGCN) exhibit extreme structural rigidity, and sequential models (SASRec) produce the highest reachability rates. CausalLens is publicly available and reproduces our full experimental pipeline on a single laptop.
